# Usability Testing

## What is Usability Testing?
**Usability Testing** is a non-functional testing technique that evaluates how easy and user-friendly a software application is. It measures the software's ease of use, learning curve, and overall user satisfaction.

---

## 5 Core Components of Usability

1. **Learnability:** How easy is it for users to accomplish basic tasks the first time they encounter the design?
2. **Efficiency:** Once users have learned the design, how quickly can they perform tasks?
3. **Memorability:** When users return to the design after a period of not using it, how easily can they reestablish proficiency?
4. **Errors:** How many errors do users make, how severe are these errors, and how easily can they recover from the errors?
5. **Satisfaction:** How pleasant is it to use the design?

---

## How to Conduct Usability Testing

1. **Identify the Target Audience:** Define the demographics (age, tech-savviness, profession) of your real users.
2. **Recruit Participants:** Gather a group of representative users (usually 5-8 users can uncover 80% of usability problems).
3. **Define Tasks:** Create realistic, goal-oriented scenarios (e.g., "Find a red shirt in size Medium and add it to your cart").
4. **Conduct Sessions:** Observe users interacting with the software. *Do not guide them.* Record their screens, clicks, and verbal feedback.
5. **Analyze and Report:** Identify pain points (e.g., users couldn't find the checkout button because it blended in with the background).

---

## Hallway Testing
A quick, informal method of usability testing where you grab random people (e.g., colleagues from other departments in the hallway) and ask them to try the software. Good for catching glaringly obvious UX flaws early.

---

## 📌 Key Takeaways
- Usability testing focuses on the **User Experience (UX)**.
- It answers the question: "Is the software intuitive?"
- Cannot be fully automated; it requires real human observation.
